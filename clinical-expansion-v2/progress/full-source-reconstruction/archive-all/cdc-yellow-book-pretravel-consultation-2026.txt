	<a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="cdc_report_pub_study_section_9-introduction" name="Introduction" title="Introduction"><!-- --></a><h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-uw2sfoz1dx">Introduction</h2>
<p>The pre-travel consultation offers a dedicated time to prepare travelers for health concerns that might arise during their trips. During the pre-travel consultation, healthcare professionals can conduct a risk assessment for each traveler, communicate risk by sharing information about potential health hazards, and manage risk by various means. Managing risk might include giving immunizations, emphasizing to travelers the importance of taking prescribed malaria prophylaxis and other medications (and highlighting the risks of not taking them correctly), and educating travelers about steps they can take to address and minimize travel-associated risks. The pre-travel consultation also serves a public health purpose by helping limit the role that international travelers may play in the global spread of infectious diseases.</p>

</div>
<div class="dfe-section" data-section="cdc_report_pub_study_section_8">
	<a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="cdc_report_pub_study_section_8-the-travel-medicine-specialist" name="The%20travel%20medicine%20specialist" title="The travel medicine specialist"><!-- --></a><h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-jipmckd_5l">The travel medicine specialist</h2>
<p>Travel medicine specialists have in-depth knowledge of immunizations, health risks associated with specific destinations, and the implications of traveling with underlying conditions. Therefore, a comprehensive consultation with a travel medicine specialist is indicated for all international travelers and is particularly important for those with a complicated health history, pregnant women, anyone taking special risks (e.g., traveling at high altitude, working in refugee camps), or those with prolonged or complicated itineraries. Healthcare professionals aspiring to be travel medicine specialists can benefit from the resources provided by the <a href="https://www.istm.org/" class="ce-selection tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="org">International Society of Travel Medicine (ISTM)<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external " aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a> and might consider <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/travel-medicine-resources-for-healthcare-professionals.html">specialty training and certification</a>.</p>

</div>
<div class="dfe-section" data-section="cdc_report_pub_study_section_5">
	<a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="cdc_report_pub_study_section_5-components-of-a-pre-travel-consultation" name="Components%20of%20a%20pre-travel%20consultation" title="Components of a pre-travel consultation"><!-- --></a><h2 class="editor-block--title" id="heading-voey6hlqcz">Components of a pre-travel consultation</h2>
<p>Effective pre-travel consultations require attention to the traveler's health background and incorporate the itinerary, trip duration, travel purpose, and activities planned during travel, which all factor into the health risk associated with travel (Box 1.1.1). The pre-travel consultation is the best opportunity to educate the traveler about health risks at the destination and how to mitigate them. The typical pre-travel consultation does not include a physical examination, and a separate appointment with the same or a different healthcare professional might be necessary to assess fitness for travel. Because travel medicine clinics are not available in some communities, primary care physicians should seek guidance from travel medicine specialists to address areas of uncertainty. The <a href="https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/travel/" class="">CDC Travelers' Health website</a> has country-specific recommendations and links to an interactive web-tool, <a href="https://www.gten.travel/prep" class="tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="travel">Pre-Travel Providers' Rapid Evaluation Portal from Global TravEpiNet<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external " aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a>, to guide primary care physicians through a pre-travel consultation.</p>
<p>Personalize travel health advice by highlighting likely exposures and reminding the traveler of ubiquitous risks (e.g., injury, foodborne and waterborne infections, vector-borne diseases, respiratory tract infections, blood-borne infections, and sexually transmitted infections). Balancing the importance of precautions with an appreciation of the positive aspects of the journey can lead to a more meaningful pre-travel consultation. In addition, pay attention to the cost of recommended interventions. It is important to recognize that some travelers are unable to afford all the recommended immunizations and medications, and to prioritize interventions in these situations (see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-for-work-other/resource-limited-settings.html" class="ce-selection">Travel Health Advice for Resource-Limited Travelers</a>).</p>
<h3 id="heading-ce5ufu5lhf">Assess individual risk</h3>
<p>Traveler characteristics and destination-specific risk provide the background to assess travel-associated health risks. Such characteristics include </p>
<ul class="nested-list">
<li class="level-1">Personal health background (e.g., past medical history, special conditions, immunization history, medications);</li>
<li class="level-1">Prior travel experience;</li>
<li class="level-1">Trip details, including itinerary, timing, reason for travel, travel style, and specific activities; and</li>
<li class="level-1">Details about the status of infectious diseases at the destination.</li>
</ul>
<p>Certain travelers also might confront special risks. Recent hospitalization for serious problems might lead to a decision to recommend delaying travel. Air travel is contraindicated for patients with certain conditions. For instance, patients should not travel by air &lt;2 weeks after an uncomplicated myocardial infarction or &lt;10 days after thoracic or abdominal surgery (see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/travelers-with-chronic-illnesses.html" class="ce-selection">Travelers with Chronic Illnesses</a>). Consult relevant healthcare professionals most familiar with the traveler's underlying conditions.</p>
<p>Other travelers with specific risks include those who have <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/travelers-with-chronic-illnesses.html" class="ce-selection">chronic illnesses</a>, are <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/immunocompromised-travelers.html" class="ce-selection">immunocompromised</a>, or are <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/family-travel/pregnant-travelers.html" class="ce-selection">pregnant</a>. Travelers <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/refugees-immigrants-migrants/visiting-friends-and-relatives-vfr-travel.html" class="ce-selection">visiting friends and relatives</a>, <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-for-work-other/long-term-travelers-and-expatriates.html" class="ce-selection">long-term travelers</a>, and travelers with <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/family-travel/traveling-safely-with-infants-and-children.html">small children</a> also face unique risks. More comprehensive discussion on advising travelers with additional health considerations is available in <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/index.html">Travelers with Additional Considerations</a>. Determine whether recent notices for outbreaks or other safety concerns have been posted for the traveler's destination by checking information available on <a href="https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/travel" class="ce-selection">CDC Travelers' Health</a>, <a href="https://www.travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/traveladvisories/traveladvisories.html/" class="ce-selection tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="gov">U.S. Department of State<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external " aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a>, and other resources.</p>
<p>In addition to recognizing the traveler's characteristics, health background, and destination-specific risks, discuss anticipated exposures related to special activities. For example, river rafting could expose a traveler to <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-associated-infections-diseases/schistosomiasis.html" class="ce-selection">schistosomiasis</a> or <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-associated-infections-diseases/leptospirosis.html" class="ce-selection">leptospirosis</a>, and spelunking in Central America could put the traveler at risk for histoplasmosis. Eating <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/food-and-water-precautions-for-travelers.html" class="ce-selection">food from street vendors</a> increases the risk for <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/travelers-diarrhea.html" class="ce-selection">travelers' diarrhea</a>. Flying from lowlands to high-elevation areas and trekking or climbing in mountainous regions introduces the risk for <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/environmental-hazards-risks/high-altitude-travel-and-altitude-illness.html" class="ce-selection">altitude illness</a>. Inquire about plans for specific leisure, <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-for-work-other/international-business-traveler.html" class="ce-selection">business</a>, and healthcare-seeking activities (<a href="/yellow-book/hcp/health-care-abroad/medical-tourism.html">medical tourism</a>).</p>
<h3 id="heading-x4nvjvfomh">Communicate risk</h3>
<p>Once destination-specific risks for a particular itinerary have been assessed, communicate them clearly to the traveler. Health-risk communication is an exchange of information in which the healthcare professional and traveler discuss potential health hazards for the trip and any available preventive measures. Communicating risk is one of the most challenging aspects of a pre-travel consultation because travelers' perceptions of and tolerance for risk can vary widely. For a more detailed discussion, see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/perspectives-travelers-perception-of-risk.html" class="ce-selection">Perspectives: Travelers' Perception of Risk</a> chapter.</p>
<h3 id="heading-6dblrpb6sj">Manage risk</h3>
<h4 id="heading-r2oat5btgy">Vaccinations</h4>
<p>Vaccinations are a central component of pre-travel consultations, and the risk assessment forms the basis of recommendations for <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/vaccination-and-immunoprophylaxis-general-principles.html" class="ce-selection">travel vaccines</a>. Consider whether the patient has sufficient time to complete a vaccine series before travel (see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/last-minute-travelers.html" class="ce-selection">Last-Minute Travelers</a>). The purpose of travel, season, duration, and specific destination within a country will inform the need for vaccines. At the same time, the pre-travel consultation presents an opportunity to update routine vaccines (Table 1.1.1).</p>
<p>Pay attention to vaccine-preventable diseases for which immunity might have waned over time or after a recent immunocompromising condition (e.g., after a hematopoietic stem cell transplant). Asking whether travelers plan to travel again in the next 1&ndash;2 years can help justify receipt of an immunization at the current visit for travel over several years (e.g., rabies pre-exposure, Japanese encephalitis, tick-borne encephalitis) rather than only the upcoming trip. When a traveler's time until departure does not allow for conventional dosing of vaccines, accelerated dosing schedules can be offered for certain vaccines (e.g., Japanese encephalitis, rabies, hepatitis B). Provide travelers with a record of administered immunizations (including the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis [i.e., Yellow Card]) and instructions to follow up as needed to complete a vaccine series. Advise travelers against obtaining medication and vaccine series in other countries; although, when necessary, completing a vaccine series at an established travel medicine center in the destination country may be considered for select vaccines.</p>

</div></div><h4 id="heading-fj3rl-6cky">Malaria prevention</h4>
<p><a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-associated-infections-diseases/malaria.html">Malaria</a> continues to cause substantial morbidity and mortality in travelers, with those visiting friends and relatives abroad accounting for the majority of cases. From 1973 to 2018, the annual number of U.S. malaria cases reported to CDC has increased; therefore, healthcare professionals should carefully assess travelers' risk for malaria and recommend preventive measures during the pre-travel consultation. For travelers going to malaria-endemic countries, discuss malaria transmission, ways to reduce risk, including mosquito-bite avoidance, recommendations for prophylaxis, symptoms of malaria, and the need for prompt medical care if symptoms develop. Additional information on malaria is available in <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/yellow-fever-vaccine-and-malaria-prevention-information-by-country.html" class="ce-selection">Yellow Fever Vaccine and Malaria Prevention Information, by Country</a>.</p>
<h4 id="heading-1ocic75sf_">Self-treatable conditions</h4>
<p>Despite healthcare professionals' best efforts, some travelers will become ill (see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/post-travel-evaluation/post-travel-evaluation-of-the-ill-traveler.html" class="ce-selection">Post-Travel Evaluation of the Ill Traveler</a>). Obtaining reliable and timely <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/health-care-abroad/what-to-do-when-sick-abroad.html">medical care during travel</a> can be problematic in many destinations. Consequently, consider prescribing certain medications in advance to enable the traveler to treat common health problems. Box 1.1.2 provides a list of some of the most common situations for which travelers find self-treatment useful.</p>
<p>Travel medicine specialists need to recognize conditions for which travelers might be at risk and provide information about appropriate self-diagnosis and treatment. Keys to a successful self-treatment strategy include: sharing a simple disease or condition definition; recommending or prescribing treatment; and educating the traveler about the expected outcome of treatment. It is important to assess the traveler's ability to manage the self-treatment plan. As an example, for travelers' diarrhea: inform travelers that most symptoms can be managed with fluid replacement plus loperamide or bismuth subsalicylate; consider prescribing travelers antibiotics they can carry with them for use in the event of incapacitating <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/travelers-diarrhea.html" class="ce-selection">diarrhea</a>; and tell them to seek medical attention if symptoms persist for 24&ndash;36 hours or are particularly severe, such as high fevers, dehydration, or bloody diarrhea.</p>
<p>With some <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travel-for-work-other/adventure-travel.html">activities in remote settings</a> (e.g., trekking), the only alternative to self-treatment would be no treatment. Pre-travel counseling might result in a more accurate self-diagnosis and treatment than relying on local medical care in some areas. In addition, the increasing awareness of substandard and counterfeit drugs in pharmacies in certain countries makes it important for travelers to bring quality manufactured drugs with them from a reliable supplier.</p>
<p>Encourage travelers to carry a travel health kit with prescription and nonprescription medications and review each traveler's medication list for possible drug-drug interactions (see <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/medication-and-vaccine-interactions-in-travel-medicine.html" class="ce-selection">Medication and Vaccine Interactions in Travel Medicine</a>; more detailed information for healthcare professionals and travelers is included in <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/preparing-international-travelers/travel-health-kits.html">Travel Health Kits</a>). <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/index.html">Travelers with Additional Considerations</a>, has supplementary travel health kit information for travelers who have additional health needs and considerations.</p>
<h4 id="heading-oel6cl2xaj">Address special health risks</h4>
<p>Travelers with <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/travelers-with-chronic-illnesses.html">underlying health conditions</a> require additional attention to health issues related to the destination and activities. For instance, travelers with a history of cardiac disease should carry medical reports, including a copy of a recent electrocardiogram. Asthma can flare in a traveler visiting a polluted city or from physical exertion during a hike; recommend that travelers discuss with their primary healthcare professional a plan for treatment and carry necessary medication in case of asthma exacerbation. Travelers should be aware of the risks associated with medical tourism (e.g., infection, procedure complications) with specific counseling for any traveler planning to seek <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/health-care-abroad/medical-tourism.html" class="ce-selection">health care during travel</a>. Advise travelers to identify any <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/travelers-with-additional-considerations/severely-allergic-travelers.html">allergies</a> or serious medical conditions on a bracelet or a card to expedite medical care in emergency situations. Section 5 provides more information on preparing for and obtaining health care abroad. Instruct travelers on how to obtain <a href="/yellow-book/hcp/health-care-abroad/travel-insurance.html">travel medical insurance</a> and direct them to resources that provide lists of reputable medical facilities at their destination, such as:</p>
<ul class="nested-list">
<li class="level-1"><a href="https://www.istm.org/" class="tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="org">International Society of Travel Medicine<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external " aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a></li>
<li class="level-1"><a href="https://www.travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/international-travel/before-you-go/your-health-abroad.html" class="ce-selection tp-link-policy" rel="noreferrer" data-domain-ext="gov">U.S. Department of State's Your Health Abroad<span class="visually-hidden sr-only" aria-label=" external icon" role="img"></span><span class="cdc-icon-external " aria-hidden="true" alt="external icon"></span></a></li>
</ul>
<h4 id="heading-hxvly9ydz_">Educate to change behavior</h4>
<p>The pre-travel consultation provides another setting to remind travelers of basic health and safety practices during travel, including frequent handwashing, wearing seatbelts, using car seats for infants and children, wearing a helmet while riding a motorcycle or bicycle, safe sexual practices, and communicable disease prevention. Organize topics into a checklist and place priority on the most serious and frequently encountered issues (Box 1.1.3). In addition, address general noninfectious issues (e.g., jet lag, sunburn). Written information is essential to supplement verbal advice and enables travelers to review the instructions from their clinic visits. <a href="https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/travel/" class="ce-selection">CDC's Travelers' Health website</a> provides educational material. By giving advice on health risks and self-treatable conditions, healthcare professionals can minimize travelers' need to seek medical care while abroad and possibly help them return to good health faster.</p>
<h3 id="heading-oq1o9wvt_k">Box 1.1.1</h3>
<div class="dfe-block cdc-callout cdc-callout--message"><div class="cdc-callout__icon" aria-hidden="true" role="img"></div>
<h3 class="cdc-callout__heading">The pre-travel consultation, medical history, and travel risk assessment</h3>
<div class="cdc-callout__body"><div class="cdc-callout__content"><p></p><p><strong>Health Background</strong></p><p><strong><em>Past Medical History</em></strong></p><p></p><ul><li>Age</li><li>Allergies (especially any pertaining to vaccines, eggs, or latex)</li><li>Medications</li><li>Sex</li><li>Underlying conditions</li></ul><strong><em>Special Conditions</em></strong><ul><li>Advanced age</li><li>Breastfeeding</li><li>Cardiopulmonary event (recent)</li><li>Cerebrovascular event (recent)</li><li>Disability or handicap</li><li>Guillain-Barr&eacute; syndrome (history of)</li><li>Immunocompromising conditions or medications</li><li>Pregnancy (including trimester)</li><li>Psychiatric condition</li><li>Seizure disorder</li><li>Surgery (recent)</li><li>Thymus abnormality</li></ul><p><strong><em>Immunization History</em></strong></p><p></p><ul><li>Routine vaccines</li><li>Travel vaccines</li></ul><strong><em>Prior Travel Experience</em></strong><p></p><ul><li>High-elevation travel/mountain climbing</li><li>Malaria chemoprophylaxis</li><li>Prior travel-related illnesses</li></ul><strong>Travel Risk Assessment (Trip Details)</strong><p><strong><em>Itinerary</em></strong></p><p></p><ul><li>Countries and specific regions, including order of countries, if &gt;1 country, and layovers</li><li>Outbreaks at destination</li><li>Rural or urban destinations</li></ul><strong><em>Timing</em></strong><p></p><ul><li>Season of travel</li><li>Time of departure</li><li>Trip duration</li></ul><strong><em>Reason for mass gathering</li><li>Tourism</li><li>Visiting friends and relatives</li><li>Volunteer, missionary, or aid work</li></ul><strong><em>Travel Style</em></strong><p></p><ul><li>Accommodations (e.g., sports</li><li>High-elevation travel</li><li>Medical care (providing or receiving)</li><li>Rafting or other water exposure</li><li>Sexual encounters (planned)</li><li>Spelunking</li><li>Substance and psychoactive experiences (including illicit drugs)</li></ul></div>
</div></div>
<h3 id="heading-ze_smumcyn">Box 1.1.2</h3>
<div class="dfe-block cdc-callout cdc-callout--message"><div class="cdc-callout__icon" aria-hidden="true" role="img"></div>
<h3 class="cdc-callout__heading">The pre-travel consultation: self-treatable conditions</h3>
<div class="cdc-callout__body"><div class="cdc-callout__content"><p>The following list includes common situations for which travelers might find self-treatment useful. The extent of self-treatment recommendations offered to travelers should reflect the remoteness and difficulty of travel, the availability of reliable medical care at the destination, and the patient's ability to manage self-treatment. Recommended self-treatment options for each of the listed diseases are provided below or in the designated sections of this text.</p><p></p><ul><li>Altitude illness</li><li>HIV exposure (occupational)</li><li>Jet lag</li><li>Malaria</li><li>Motion sickness</li><li>Travelers' diarrhea</li><li>Urinary tract infections: Common among many people; carrying a prescribed antibiotic for empiric treatment can be helpful, particularly in persons with a history of recurrent urinary tract infections. </li><li>Vaginal yeast infections: Self-treatment course of patient's preferred antifungal medication can be prescribed for women who are prone to infections, sexually active, or who might be receiving antibiotics for other reasons, including doxycycline for malaria chemoprophylaxis.</li></ul></div>
</div></div>
<h3 id="heading-cvfp67t-xw">Box 1.1.3</h3>
<div class="dfe-block cdc-callout cdc-callout--message"><div class="cdc-callout__icon" aria-hidden="true" role="img"></div>
<h3 class="cdc-callout__heading">The pre-travel consultation, key discussion topics</h3>
<div class="cdc-callout__body"><div class="cdc-callout__content"><p></p><p><strong>Altitude illness</strong><br></p><ul><li>Determine if the itinerary puts the traveler at risk of altitude illness.</li><li>Discuss preventive measures (e.g., gradual ascent, adequate hydration, medications to prevent and treat).</li></ul><strong>Blood-borne pathogens</strong><br><ul><li>Advise travelers to avoid potential exposures (e.g., injections, piercings, tattoos, shared razors).</li><li>Inform travelers who will provide health care overseas and what to do in case of needlesticks or blood-borne pathogen exposures; discuss use of HIV post-exposure prophylaxis.</li></ul><strong>Disease-specific counseling</strong><br><ul><li>Advise travelers to prepare for exacerbations or complications from underlying disease(s).</li><li>Remind travelers to keep medications and supplies in carry-on luggage, to keep medications in their original prescription bottles, and to carry copies of their written prescriptions.</li><li>Provide a letter and medication list for patients with controlled substances or polypharmacy.</li></ul><strong>Environmental hazards</strong><br><ul><li>Advise travelers to avoid walking barefoot to reduce their chances of certain parasitic infections.</li><li>Advise travelers to avoid wading or swimming in freshwater where risk for schistosomiasis or leptospirosis is possible.</li><li>Caution travelers to avoid contact with animals to reduce the potential for bites and scratches that can transmit rabies. This is particularly important advice for the parents of young children.</li><li>Remind travelers to apply sunscreen to sun-exposed skin.</li><li>Assess for air quality concerns at the destination and have travelers plan accordingly (e.g., masks, restricting physical activity, carrying medications).</li></ul><strong>Immunizations</strong><br><ul><li>Discuss indications for, effectiveness of, and adverse reactions to immunizations.</li><li>Discuss benefit of antibody titers when past vaccine records are unavailable or unreliable, particularly for hepatitis A, measles, mumps, rubella, and varicella.</li><li>Review routine immunizations and travel immunizations indicated for the specific itinerary and based on the traveler's medical history.</li></ul><strong>Malaria, yellow fever, and other vector-borne diseases</strong><br><ul><li>Define vector-borne disease risks at the destination.</li><li>Discuss personal protective measures and recommended insect bite precautions.</li><li>For itineraries where malaria transmission is a risk: discuss risks and benefits of malaria chemoprophylaxis and recommended chemoprophylaxis choices. For itineraries where yellow fever virus transmission is a risk: assess individual traveler precautions and contraindications for receiving yellow fever vaccine; discuss risks and benefits of vaccination; and discuss alternatives to vaccination for travelers at increased risk for adverse events from yellow fever vaccine.</li></ul><strong>Personal safety</strong><br><ul><li>Advise travelers to look for security bulletins related to their destination and consider areas to avoid.</li><li>Discuss precautions that travelers can take (including avoiding excess alcohol consumption) to minimize risk for traffic accidents, personal assault, robbery, or drowning.</li><li>Provide information on travel health and medical evacuation insurance.</li><li>If attending a mass gathering, advise about ensuring physical safety and remaining aware of their surroundings.</li></ul><strong>Respiratory illnesses</strong><br><ul><li>Discuss influenza and COVID-19 prevention, including optimizing vaccination.</li><li>Discuss diseases, destinations of particular concern, and management strategies.</li></ul><strong>Sexual health and sexually transmitted infections</strong><br><ul><li>Discuss recommended vaccinations, including those that protect against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).</li><li>Discuss pre-exposure prophylaxis medication for HIV for high-risk travelers. Advise on consistent and correct use of condoms to decrease the risk of HIV and STIs.</li><li>Discuss post-exposure prophylaxis against HIV or unintended pregnancy within 72 hours after a high-risk sexual encounter.</li><li>Advise to avoid engaging in sex with a minor (&lt;18 years old), child pornography, or trafficking activities in any country.</li><li>Advise to avoid exposing sexual partners at home and to seek evaluation for recommended tests for HIV and STIs.</li></ul><strong>Travelers' diarrhea and other food- and water-borne illnesses</strong><br><ul><li>Discuss food and water safety.</li><li>Discuss self-treatment of diarrhea with anti-motility agents, hydration, and when necessary, antibiotics.</li><li>Recommend strategies to decrease risk of diarrhea.</li></ul></div>
</div></div>
