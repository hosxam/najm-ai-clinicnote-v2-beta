<div class="chapter" title="Recommendations for research" id="ng253-recommendations-for-research" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml">
  <h2 class="title" id="recommendations-for-research">Recommendations for research</h2>
  <div class="section" title="Key recommendations for research" id="ng253-key-recommendations-for-research">
    <h3 class="title" id="key-recommendations-for-research">Key recommendations for research</h3>
    <div class="section research-recommendation" title="1 Epidemiological study on presentation and management of sepsis in England" id="ng253-epidemiological-study-on-presentation-and-management-of-sepsis-in-england">
      <h4 class="title" id="epidemiological-study-on-presentation-and-management-of-sepsis-in-england">1 Epidemiological study on presentation and management of sepsis in England</h4>
      <p>What is the incidence, presentation and management of sepsis in the United Kingdom? <strong>[2016]</strong></p>
      <div class="section" title="Why this is important" id="ng253-why-this-is-important">
        <h5 class="title" id="why-this-is-important">Why this is important</h5>
        <p>The lack of robust UK based epidemiological studies on the incidence and outcomes from sepsis have been clear throughout the guideline development process. A large epidemiological study to collect information about where sepsis is being treated, patient interventions and patient outcomes would provide population-based statistics on epidemiology of sepsis which are necessary to support evaluation of interventions, planning of services and service redesign. The mortality and morbidity and service complexity associated with severe infection and sepsis, and the need to use broad-spectrum antimicrobials to treat sepsis, justifies the cost required to set up such a study.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="section research-recommendation" title="2 Association between NEWS2 bands (0, 1 to 4, 5 to 6, 7 or above) and risk of severe illness or death" id="ng253-association-between-news2-bands-0-1-to-4-5-to-6-7-or-above-and-risk-of-severe-illness-or">
      <h4 class="title" id="association-between-news2-bands-0-1-to-4-5-to-6-7-or-above-and-risk-of-severe-illness-or">2 Association between NEWS2 bands (0, 1 to 4, 5 to 6, 7 or above) and risk of severe illness or death</h4>
      <p>In adults and young people (16 and over) with suspected sepsis in acute hospital settings, ambulance trusts and acute mental health facilities, what is the association between NEWS2 bands (0, 1 to 4, 5 to 6, 7 or above) and risk of severe illness or death? In adults and young people (16 and over) with suspected sepsis in acute hospital settings, ambulance trusts and acute mental health facilities, what is the association between the NEWS2 score of 3 in a single parameter and risk of severe illness or death? <strong>[2024]</strong></p>
      <div class="section" title="Why this is important" id="ng253-why-this-is-important-2">
        <h5 class="title" id="why-this-is-important-2">Why this is important</h5>
        <p>The NEWS2 has been introduced in 2017 and is widely used across the NHS pre-hospital and acute care settings. However, evidence on the NEWS2 was not found. It is important to investigate, over a 5- to 10‑year period, the success, safety and possible implications on people with suspected sepsis and clinical staff of using the NEWS2 to stratify the risk of severe illness or death from sepsis.</p>
        <p>Lack of data to stratify risk of severe illness or death from sepsis and estimate possible risk of deterioration in people with a single parameter contributing 3 points to their NEWS2 score is also of great concern. Data relating to this is scarce and its interpretation contradictory.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="section research-recommendation" title="3 Derivation of clinical decision rules in suspected sepsis" id="ng253-derivation-of-clinical-decision-rules-in-suspected-sepsis">
      <h4 class="title" id="derivation-of-clinical-decision-rules-in-suspected-sepsis">3 Derivation of clinical decision rules in suspected sepsis</h4>
      <p>Is it possible to derive and validate a set of clinical decision rules or a predictive tool to rule out sepsis which can be applied to patients presenting to hospital with suspected sepsis? <strong>[2016]</strong></p>
      <div class="section" title="Why this is important" id="ng253-why-this-is-important-3">
        <h5 class="title" id="why-this-is-important-3">Why this is important</h5>
        <p>In primary care and emergency departments people with suspected sepsis are often seen by relatively inexperienced doctors. Many of these people will be in low and medium risk groups but evidence is lacking as to who can be sent home safely and who needs intravenous or oral antibiotics. The consequences of getting the decision making wrong can be catastrophic and therefore many patients are potentially over-investigated and admitted inappropriately. Current guidance is dependent on use of individual variables informed by low quality evidence.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="section" title="Other recommendations for research" id="ng253-other-recommendations-for-research">
    <h3 class="title" id="other-recommendations-for-research">Other recommendations for research</h3>
    <div class="section research-recommendation" title="4 Rapid microbiological testing" id="ng253-rapid-microbiological-testing">
      <h4 class="title" id="rapid-microbiological-testing">4 Rapid microbiological testing</h4>
      <p>How can rapid microbiological testing guide management in people with suspected sepsis? This should include:</p>
      <div class="itemizedlist">
        <ul class="itemizedlist">
          <li class="listitem">
            <p>consideration of good antimicrobial stewardship</p>
          </li>
          <li class="listitem">
            <p>the clinical and cost effectiveness of the tests</p>
          </li>
          <li class="listitem">
            <p>the time taken to do the test and get a result. <strong>[2025]</strong></p>
          </li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="section" title="Why this is important" id="ng253-why-this-is-important-4">
        <h5 class="title" id="why-this-is-important-4">Why this is important</h5>
        <p>Diagnosing and treating an underlying infection could prevent serious illness or death in a person who has or is at risk of developing sepsis. However, starting treatment too early could lead to people without an infection being given antibiotics, or people being given inappropriate antibiotics for their infection.</p>
        <p>Early use of rapid microbiological tests could help diagnose infection faster, leading to quicker, more targeted treatment and better patient outcomes. These tests are currently used in the NHS, but more research is needed to measure their utility in people with suspected sepsis.</p>
        <div class="panel">
          <p>For a short explanation of why the committee made the 2024 recommendation and how it might affect practice, see the <a class="link" href="rationale-and-impact#rapid-antigen-and-rapid-pcr-testing" target="_top" rel="internal" data-chapter="rationale-and-impact" data-original-url="#rapid-antigen-and-rapid-pcr-testing">rationale section on rapid antigen and rapid PCR testing</a>.</p>
          <p>Full details of the evidence and the committee's discussion are in:</p>
          <div class="itemizedlist">
            <ul class="itemizedlist">
              <li class="listitem">
                <p>
                  <a class="link" href="https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng253/evidence/d-rapid-tests-for-assessing-infection-in-people-with-suspected-sepsis-pdf-15494509406" target="_top">evidence review D: rapid tests for assessing infection in people with suspected sepsis</a>
                </p>
              </li>
              <li class="listitem">
                <p>
                  <a class="link" href="https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng253/evidence/e-clinical-and-cost-effectiveness-of-rapid-antigen-tests-rat-and-polymerase-chain-reaction-pcr-pdf-15494509407" target="_top">evidence review E: clinical and cost effectiveness of rapid antigen tests (RAT) and polymerase chain reaction (PCR) tests for guiding treatment in people with suspected sepsis</a>.</p>
              </li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="section research-recommendation" title="5 Vasopressors" id="ng253-vasopressors-2">
      <h4 class="title" id="vasopressors-2">5 Vasopressors</h4>
      <p>In people assessed as being at moderate or high risk of severe illness or death from suspected sepsis, how safe is the peripheral administration of different infusion durations, doses and concentrations of vasopressors? <strong>[2025]</strong></p>
      <div class="section" title="Why this is important" id="ng253-why-this-is-important-5">
        <h5 class="title" id="why-this-is-important-5">Why this is important</h5>
        <p>Vasopressors are part of the treatment for hypotension and hypotensive shock in people with sepsis. Research is needed into the safety of administering these peripherally. This includes details on the safety of different durations, doses and concentrations, which would give clinicians more information to make decisions on whether to start vasopressors peripherally when these are needed.</p>
        <div class="panel">
          <p>For a short explanation of why the committee made the 2024 recommendation and how it might affect practice, see the <a class="link" href="rationale-and-impact#vasopressors-3" target="_top" rel="internal" data-chapter="rationale-and-impact" data-original-url="#vasopressors-3">rationale section on vasopressors</a>.</p>
          <p>Full details of the evidence and the committee's discussion are in <a class="link" href="https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng253/evidence/h-safety-of-peripheral-administration-of-vasopressor-pdf-15494509410" target="_top">evidence review H: safety of peripheral administration of vasopressor</a>.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
